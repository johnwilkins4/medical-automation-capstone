# Upper Respiratory Infection (URI / Common Cold)

## Overview
Upper respiratory infections are the most common reason for outpatient visits. The vast majority are viral — rhinoviruses account for 30–50%, followed by coronaviruses, influenza, RSV, adenovirus, and parainfluenza. The ICD-10 code **J06.9** ("acute upper respiratory infection, unspecified") is the most frequent billing code.

## Symptoms
Sore throat, rhinorrhea, nasal congestion, mild fever (< 101 °F), cough, sneezing, malaise. Symptoms peak at day 2–3 and resolve within 7–10 days in healthy adults.

## When to suspect something other than a cold
- High fever (> 101.5 °F), especially with rigors.
- Severe unilateral facial pain or pressure (bacterial sinusitis).
- Purulent tonsillar exudate + tender anterior cervical nodes + fever + absence of cough (strep pharyngitis — Centor score).
- Productive cough with dyspnea or chest pain (lower respiratory infection, pneumonia).
- Symptoms lasting > 10 days without improvement (bacterial superinfection).

## Treatment
Supportive care — no antivirals indicated for the common cold in most patients.

- **Rest and hydration.**
- **Analgesics / antipyretics:** acetaminophen (paracetamol) or ibuprofen for fever and body aches.
- **Nasal saline irrigation** for congestion.
- **Decongestants** (pseudoephedrine, oxymetazoline — limit nasal spray to 3 days).
- **Antihistamines** (first-gen like chlorpheniramine) may reduce rhinorrhea.
- **Honey** (in adults and children > 1 year) is evidence-based for cough suppression.

## What does NOT work
Antibiotics are not indicated for uncomplicated viral URIs. Vitamin C and zinc have small, inconsistent effects. Cough and cold syrups are not recommended in children < 6.

## When to follow up
- Symptoms not improving after 5–7 days
- Symptoms worsening after initial improvement
- Development of dyspnea, chest pain, high fever, or purulent sputum
